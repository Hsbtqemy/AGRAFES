THE PRINCE
Niccolò Machiavelli (1532)
Translation by Ninian Hill Thomson (1882) — Public domain
Language: English — Role: translation

DEDICATION TO LORENZO DE' MEDICI

[1] It is customary for those who wish to gain the favour of a prince to endeavour to do so by offering him gifts of the things which they hold most precious, or in which they know him to take especial delight.
[2] In this way princes are often presented with horses, arms, cloth of gold, gems, and such-like costly presents worthy of their grandeur.
[3] Desiring in like manner to present myself to your Magnificence with some token of my devotion, I have found among my possessions none that I prize or esteem so highly as the knowledge of the actions of great men, which I have acquired through long experience of modern affairs and a continual study of ancient history.
[4] The results of my long observations and reflections are recorded in the little work now offered to your Magnificence.
[5] And although I have carefully revised and arranged this volume, I send it to your Magnificence without further care for its reception.
[6] I hope it may not be counted presumptuous for a man of humble position to attempt to discuss and direct the conduct of princes; for as those who draw maps of countries place themselves low down in the plain to study the character of mountains and elevated places, and ascend the mountains to get a better view of the plains, so in like manner to understand the people a man should be a prince, and to have a clear notion of princes he should belong to the people.
[7] May your Magnificence then accept this little gift in the spirit in which it is offered; and if your Magnificence will deign to peruse it, you will recognise in it my ardent desire that you may attain to that greatness which fortune and your own merits predict for you.

CHAPTER I — How many kinds of principalities there are, and by what means they are acquired

[8] All states and dominions which hold or have held sway over mankind are either republics or principalities.
[9] Principalities are either hereditary, in which the rulers have been for many years of the same family, or else they are of recent foundation.
[10] The newly founded ones are either entirely new, as was Milan to Francesco Sforza, or they are, as it were, new members grafted on to the hereditary state of the prince who acquires them, as was the kingdom of Naples to the King of Spain.
[11] Such dominions thus acquired are either accustomed to live under a prince, or are free states; and are annexed either by force of arms of the prince himself, or of others, or else by fortune or by merit.
[12] These states are acquired either with the arms of others or with one's own, either by fortune or by merit.

CHAPTER II — Of hereditary principalities

[13] I will not speak of republics, as I have treated of them at length elsewhere.
[14] I will address myself only to principalities, and following up the thread already started, I will discuss how these principalities can be governed and maintained.
[15] I say, then, that hereditary states, accustomed to the rule of the prince's family, are maintained with far less difficulty than new ones.
[16] For it suffices that the prince does not exceed the usage of his ancestors, temporising with circumstances when necessity arises.
[17] In this way, if he is of average ability, he will always be able to maintain his position, unless some unusual or excessive force deprives him of it.
[18] And if he should be so deprived, on the slightest mischance happening to the new occupier, he will be able to regain it.
[19] We have in Italy, as an example, the Duke of Ferrara, who was unable to resist the attacks of the Venetians in 1484, nor those of Pope Julius in 1510, for no other reasons than that he was well established in his domain.
[20] For the natural prince has fewer occasions and less necessity to give offence; wherefore it is fitting that he should be better loved, and unless extraordinary vices make him hated, it is reasonable that he should naturally have the goodwill of his subjects.
[21] For the antiquity and continuity of the rule causes the memories and causes of innovations to be forgotten, inasmuch as one change always leaves the toothing for another.

CHAPTER III — Of mixed principalities

[22] But it is the new principality that gives rise to difficulties. In the first place, if it is not entirely new, but an appendage to an old state, so that the whole may be called composite, its troubles spring from one natural difficulty which exists in all new principalities.
[23] Men willingly change their lord, hoping thereby to better their condition; and this hope induces them to take up arms against him who rules.
[24] In this they are deceived, because they afterwards find by experience that they are worse off.
[25] This results from another natural and ordinary necessity, which always causes a new prince to harm those whose prince he has become.
[26] He harms them by billeting soldiers, by levying contributions, and by innumerable other wrongs attendant upon a new conquest, so that he has all of them for enemies who have been injured by him in occupying the principality.
[27] And he is unable to keep as friends those who have assisted him in obtaining it, as he cannot satisfy their expectations nor can he employ strong remedies against them, being bound to them.
[28] For, however strong a prince may be in armies, he will always have need of the support of the natives to enter a new province.
[29] For these reasons Louis XII of France rapidly occupied Milan and as rapidly lost it.

CHAPTER IV — Why the kingdom of Darius, occupied by Alexander, did not rebel against the successors of Alexander after his death

[30] The difficulties which were experienced in holding the newly conquered state of Alexander the Great, who conquered Asia in a few years and died before he had well established it, may occasion a doubt: for it might be thought reasonable that the whole empire would have revolted on his death.
[31] Nevertheless his successors maintained themselves and had no difficulty in holding it except that which arose among themselves from their own ambitions.
[32] I reply that principalities of which one has record are governed in two different ways.
[33] Either by a prince with a body of servants, who assist him to govern the kingdom as ministers by his favour and permission.
[34] Or by a prince and barons, who hold their position by antiquity of blood and not by the grace of the prince.

CHAPTER V — How cities or principalities should be governed that, before being occupied, had lived under their own laws

[35] When those states which have been acquired are accustomed to live at liberty under their own laws, there are three ways of holding them.
[36] The first is to devastate them; the second is to go and live there in person; the third is to allow them to live under their own laws, drawing a tribute, and establishing within it an oligarchy which will keep it friendly to you.
[37] For such a government, being created by the prince, knows that it cannot stand without his friendship and interest, and does its utmost to support him; and therefore he who would keep a city accustomed to freedom will hold it more easily by the means of its own citizens than in any other way.
[38] In illustration of this are the Spartans and the Romans. The Spartans held Athens and Thebes, establishing there an oligarchy; nevertheless they lost them.
[39] The Romans, in order to hold Capua, Carthage, and Numantia, dismantled them, and did not lose them.

CHAPTER VI — Of new principalities acquired by one's own arms and prowess

[40] Let no one wonder if, in speaking of principalities wholly new as to their prince and constitution, I bring forward the highest examples; for as men walk almost always in paths beaten by others, and make their actions by imitation, being unable to follow exactly the ways of others or to attain to the merit of those whom you imitate, a wise man should always follow the paths beaten by great men, and imitate those who have been most excellent.
[41] A prudent man should therefore enter upon the paths trodden by great men and imitate those who have been most excellent, so that if his merit does not equal theirs, at least it will savour of it.
[42] I say, then, that in entirely new principalities, where there is a new prince, there is more or less difficulty in maintaining them, according to the greater or lesser ability of him who has acquired them.
[43] And as this fact of becoming a prince from a private station presupposes either ability or fortune, it is clear that one or other of these things will mitigate in some degree many difficulties.
[44] Nevertheless, he who has relied least on fortune is established the strongest.
[45] It is also facilitated by the prince being compelled to reside personally in the new state, having no other territories.

CHAPTER VII — Of new principalities acquired by the power and arms of others

[46] Those who rise from private individuals to be princes by mere fortune have little trouble in rising but very much in maintaining their position.
[47] They have no difficulties on the way as they fly over them, but all their difficulties arise when they have arrived.
[48] Such are those who are given a state either for money or by the favour of him who grants it, as happened to many in Greece in the cities of Ionia and the Hellespont, where they were made princes by Darius, so that he might hold these places for his own security and glory.
[49] Such also are those who by corrupt means come to be emperors from private stations.
[50] These stand simply on the goodwill and fortune of those who have raised them — two things most volatile and unstable.
[51] They have neither knowledge nor ability to maintain such a position. They have not the knowledge, because unless they are men of great ability and virtue, it is not likely that one who has always lived in a private station should know how to command.
[52] They have not the ability, because they have no troops that are friendly and faithful to them.
[53] Moreover, states that rise rapidly, just as all other things in nature which grow quickly, cannot have such roots and correspondences as will make the first shock of adversity fruitless.
[54] Unless, as is said, those who so unexpectedly become princes are of so much ability that they know right away how to prepare to keep what fortune has thrown into their laps, and afterwards to build up those foundations which others lay before becoming princes.

CHAPTER VIII — Of those who have attained the position of prince by villainy

[55] But as there are also two other ways of becoming a prince which cannot entirely be attributed either to fortune or to merit, I do not wish to pass them over, although one could be more amply discussed when I treat of republics.
[56] These are when one becomes a prince by some nefarious or villainous means, or when a private citizen becomes the prince of his country by the favour of his fellow-citizens.
[57] And speaking of the first method, it will be illustrated by two examples, one ancient and one modern, without entering further into the subject as I consider it sufficient for any one who has to imitate them.
[58] Agathocles the Sicilian, not only from a private but from a low and abject position, became King of Syracuse.
[59] This man, the son of a potter, through all the stages of his fortune always led a wicked life.
[60] Nevertheless, he accompanied his wickedness with so much ability of mind and body, that, having devoted himself to the military profession, he rose through its ranks to be praetor of Syracuse.
[61] Being established in that position, and having determined to become prince and to seize by violence and without obligation to others what had been conceded to him by consent, he came to an understanding with Hamilcar the Carthaginian, who was fighting with his forces in Sicily; and one morning he assembled the people and the senate of Syracuse.
[62] At a given signal the soldiers killed all the senators and the richest of the people; after which he seized and held the princedom of that city without any civil strife.

CHAPTER IX — Of the civic principality

[63] But coming now to the other alternative, when a leading citizen becomes the prince of his country, not by villainy or any intolerable violence, but by the favour of his fellow citizens — this may be called a civic principality.
[64] I say that one attains this principality either by the favour of the people or by the favour of the nobles.
[65] For in all cities these two different parties are to be found, arising from the desire of the people to avoid the oppression of the great, and the desire of the great to command and oppress the people.
[66] For in every city these two different parties are found, arising from the desire of the people not to be commanded or oppressed by the nobles, and the desire of the nobles to command and oppress the people.
[67] And from these two opposing desires there arises in cities one of three results: either a principality, or liberty, or licence.
[68] A principality is created either by the people or by the nobles, according as either one or the other of them has the opportunity.
[69] For the nobles, seeing they cannot withstand the people, begin to cry up the reputation of one of themselves, and they make him a prince, so that under his shadow they can give vent to their ambitions.
[70] The people, finding they cannot resist the nobles, also cry up the reputation of one of themselves, and make him a prince so as to be defended by his authority.

CHAPTER X — How the strength of all principalities ought to be measured

[71] In examining the character of these principalities it is necessary to consider another point, namely, whether a prince has power sufficient to stand alone if necessary, or whether he is always in need of the protection of others.
[72] And to make this quite clear I say that I consider those who are able to stand by themselves who can, through abundance either of men or money, raise a sufficient army to join battle against any who comes to attack them.
[73] And I consider those always to have need of others who cannot show themselves against the enemy in the field, but are forced to retreat behind walls, and to defend them.
[74] The first case has been discussed, and we shall say what is necessary for it in the future.
[75] In the second case there is nothing to advise except to encourage such princes to provision and fortify their towns, and not on any account to defend the country.
[76] And whoever shall fortify his town well, and shall have managed the other concerns of his subjects in the way stated above, and to be often repeated, will never be attacked without great caution.
[77] For men are always averse to enterprises where difficulties can be foreseen, and it will be seen that an attack on one who has fortified his town, who is not hated by his people, is not easy.

CHAPTER XI — Of ecclesiastical principalities

[78] It now only remains to speak of ecclesiastical principalities, with regard to which all difficulties occur before they are possessed.
[79] They are acquired either by ability or by fortune, but are maintained without either, for they are sustained by ancient ordinances of religion, which are so powerful and of such quality that they keep their princes in power in whatever manner they proceed and live.
[80] These princes alone have states and do not defend them, subjects and do not govern them; yet their states, although undefended, are not taken from them; their subjects, although not governed, do not resent it, and neither think of, nor are able to, alienate themselves from them.
[81] Only these principalities, therefore, are secure and happy.
[82] But being upheld by higher causes than the human mind can attain to, I will not presume to speak of them; for being exalted and maintained by God, it would be the act of a presumptuous and rash man to discuss them.
[83] I will speak only of those which are human, and will leave the divine ones, as being set up and maintained by God, for theologians to discuss.

CHAPTER XII — How many kinds of soldiery there are, and concerning mercenaries

[84] Having discussed at length all the qualities of such principalities as in the beginning I proposed to examine, and having considered in some degree the causes of their being good or bad, and having shown the methods by which many have sought to acquire and hold them, it now remains for me to discuss generally the means of offence and defence which belong to each of them.
[85] We have seen above how necessary it is for a prince to have his foundations well laid, otherwise it follows of necessity he will go to ruin. The chief foundations of all states, new as well as old or composite, are good laws and good arms.
[86] And as there cannot be good laws where the state is not well armed, it follows that where they are well armed they have good laws. I shall leave the laws out of the discussion and shall talk of the arms.
[87] I say, therefore, that the arms with which a prince defends his state are either his own, or they are mercenaries, auxiliaries, or mixed.
[88] Mercenaries and auxiliaries are useless and dangerous; and if one holds his state based on these arms, he will stand neither firm nor safe; for they are disunited, ambitious, and without discipline, unfaithful, valiant before friends, cowardly before enemies.
[89] They have neither the fear of God nor fidelity to men, and destruction is deferred only so long as the attack is; for in peace one is robbed by them and in war by the enemy.
[90] The fact is that they have no other attraction or reason for keeping the field than a trifle of stipend, which is not sufficient to make them willing to die for you.
[91] They are ready enough to be your soldiers whilst you do not make war, but if war comes they take themselves off or run from the foe.
[92] The cause of this is that they have no other love or other reason to keep them in the field than a small pay, which is not sufficient to make them want to die for you.

CHAPTER XIII — Of auxiliary, mixed, and native troops

[93] Auxiliaries, which are the other useless arm, are employed when a prince is called in with his forces to aid and defend you.
[94] Pope Julius did this in recent times, for in the enterprise against Ferrara, having proved the worthlessness of his mercenary troops, he turned to auxiliaries, and stipulated with Ferdinand, King of Spain, for his assistance with men and arms.
[95] These arms may be useful and good in themselves, but for him who calls them in they are almost always injurious.
[96] For losing, one is undone, and winning, one is their captive.
[97] And although ancient histories may be full of examples, I do not wish to leave this recent one of Pope Julius II, which is still fresh.
[98] A wise prince, therefore, will always avoid these arms and turn to his own; and will prefer sooner to lose with them than to win with others, not counting that a true victory which is gained with the arms of others.
[99] He should prefer to lose with his own than to win with those of others, not counting that a real victory which is won with alien arms.

CHAPTER XIV — What the duties of a prince are with regard to the militia

[100] A prince ought to have no other aim or thought, nor select anything else for his study, than war and its rules and discipline; for this is the sole art that belongs to him who rules.
[101] And it partakes of such a nature that it not only maintains those who are born princes, but it often enables men to rise from a private station to that rank.
[102] And, on the contrary, it is seen that when princes have thought more of ease than of arms they have lost their states.
[103] And the first cause of your losing it is to neglect this art; and what enables you to acquire a state is to be master of this art.
[104] Sforza, because he was armed, from a commoner became Duke of Milan; his sons, because they avoided the inconveniences of arms, from dukes became commoners.

CHAPTER XV — Of the things for which men, and especially princes, are praised or blamed

[105] It now remains to be seen what are the methods and rules for a prince as regards his subjects and friends.
[106] And as I know that many have written on this point, I expect I shall be considered presumptuous in mentioning it again, especially as in discussing it I shall depart from the methods of other people.
[107] But my intention being to write something of use to those who understand it, it appears to me more appropriate to follow up the real truth of the matter than the imagination of it.
[108] For many have pictured republics and principalities which in fact have never been known or seen, because how one lives is so far distant from how one ought to live.
[109] That a man who neglects what is actually done for what should be done learns the way to self-destruction rather than self-preservation.
[110] Therefore it is necessary for a prince who wishes to maintain himself to learn how not to be good, and to use this knowledge or not according to necessity.

CHAPTER XVI — Of liberality and niggardliness

[111] Commencing then with the first qualities above named, I say that it would be well to be reputed generous.
[112] Nevertheless, generosity exercised in a way that does not bring you the reputation for it, injures you.
[113] For if one exercises it honestly and as it should be exercised, it may not become known, and you will not avoid the reproach of its opposite.
[114] And therefore anyone wishing to maintain among men the name of liberal is obliged to avoid no attribute of magnificence.
[115] So that a prince thus inclined will consume in such acts all his property, and will be compelled in the end, if he wish to maintain the name of liberal, to unduly weigh down his people, and tax them, and do everything he can to get money.
[116] This will soon make him odious to his subjects, and becoming poor he will be little valued by any one.
[117] Thus, with his liberality, having offended many and rewarded few, he is affected by the very first trouble and imperilled by whatever may be the first danger.
[118] Recognising this himself, and wishing to draw back from it, he runs at once into the reproach of being miserly.

CHAPTER XVII — Of cruelty and clemency, and whether it is better to be loved than feared

[119] Coming now to the other qualities mentioned above, I say that every prince ought to desire to be considered clement and not cruel.
[120] Nevertheless he ought to take care not to misuse this clemency.
[121] Cesare Borgia was considered cruel; notwithstanding, his cruelty reconciled the Romagna, unified it, and restored it to peace and loyalty.
[122] If this be rightly considered, he will be seen to have been much more merciful than the Florentine people, who, to avoid a reputation for cruelty, permitted Pistoia to be destroyed.
[123] Therefore a prince, so long as he keeps his subjects united and loyal, ought not to mind the reproach of cruelty.
[124] Because with a few examples he will be more merciful than those who, through too much mercy, allow disorders to arise, from which follow murder or robbery.
[125] For these things harm the whole community, while those executions which originate with a prince offend the individual only.
[126] And it is impossible for the new prince, above all other princes, to avoid the imputation of cruelty, owing to new states being full of dangers.
[127] Hence arises a question: whether it be better to be loved than feared or feared than loved.
[128] It may be answered that one should wish to be both, but because it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer to be feared than loved, when one of the two must be absent.
[129] Because of men it may generally be affirmed that they are thankless, fickle, false, cowardly, covetous.
[130] And as long as you succeed they are yours entirely; they will offer you their blood, property, life, and children, as is said above, when the need is far distant.
[131] But when it approaches they turn against you. And that prince who, relying entirely on their promises, has neglected other precautions, is ruined.
[132] For friendships that are obtained by payments, and not by greatness or nobility of mind, may indeed be earned, but they are not secured, and in time of need cannot be relied upon.
[133] Men have less scruple in offending one who is beloved than one who is feared.
[134] For love is preserved by the link of obligation which, owing to the baseness of men, is broken at every opportunity for their advantage.
[135] But fear preserves you by a dread of punishment which never fails.
[136] Nevertheless a prince ought to inspire fear in such a way that, if he does not win love, he avoids hatred.

CHAPTER XVIII — In what way princes should keep faith

[137] Every one admits how praiseworthy it is in a prince to keep faith, and to live with integrity and not with craft.
[138] Nevertheless our experience has been that those princes who have done great things have held good faith of little account, and have known how to circumvent the intellect of men by craft.
[139] You must know there are two ways of contesting, the one by the law, the other by force.
[140] The first method is proper to men, the second to beasts.
[141] But because the first is frequently not sufficient, it is necessary to have recourse to the second.
[142] Therefore it is necessary for a prince to understand how to avail himself of the beast and the man.
[143] A prince, therefore, being compelled knowingly to adopt the beast, ought to choose the fox and the lion; because the lion cannot defend himself against snares, and the fox cannot defend himself against wolves.
[144] Therefore, it is necessary to be a fox to discover the snares and a lion to terrify the wolves.
[145] Those who rely simply on the lion do not understand what they are about.
[146] Therefore a wise lord cannot, nor ought he to, keep faith when such observance may be turned against him, and when the reasons that caused him to pledge it exist no longer.
[147] If men were entirely good this precept would not hold, but because they are bad, and will not keep faith with you, you too are not bound to observe it with them.
[148] Nor will there ever be wanting to a prince legitimate reasons to excuse this non-observance.
